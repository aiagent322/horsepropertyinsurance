What Your Horse Property Insurance Does Not Cover

The moment you charge someone money for boarding, lessons, or training — your homeowners and property policy stops protecting you. This is not a technicality. It is a deliberate, specifically written exclusion.

🚨
This Is Not a Gray Area

Standard homeowners and farm-dwelling policies contain explicit business activity exclusions. These are not technicalities insurers use to avoid claims — they are fundamental coverage boundaries that define what the policy was designed to cover. If you operate any business from your horse property, you need to read every word on this page.

The Business Activity Exclusion — How It Works

Nearly every standard homeowners and residential property policy contains language similar to this:

"This policy does not apply to bodily injury or property damage arising out of business pursuits of any insured or the rental or holding for rental of any part of any premises by any insured."

That language appears in some form in virtually every standard policy. The key term is "business pursuits" — and courts have consistently interpreted it broadly. Charging $400/month for board is a business pursuit. Giving a $50 riding lesson is a business pursuit. Breeding a mare for a fee is a business pursuit. Training someone's horse for a day rate is a business pursuit.

The 12 Things Your Property Policy Does Not Cover

These are the specific gaps that appear in horse property insurance claims — consistently, reliably, and expensively.

1. Liability for Client Horses in Your Care

Care, custody, and control (CCC) exclusions in standard liability policies exclude coverage for property in your care. A client's horse is property in your care. If that horse is injured, dies, or is lost while in your custody — whether from a barn fire, an equipment malfunction, a kick from another horse, or improper care — your personal liability policy will not respond. You need a specific commercial equine liability policy with CCC coverage.

2. Bodily Injury to Paying Clients or Students

If a student falls during a lesson at your facility, a boarding client is injured handling their own horse in your barn, or a visitor to your operation is hurt — your homeowners liability excludes injuries on business premises during business activities. A personal umbrella policy typically follows the same exclusion. Commercial general liability coverage is required.

3. Employee Injuries (Workers Compensation)

If you pay anyone to work on your horse property — barn help, a groom, a part-time trainer, a farrier you employ — you have employees. In most states, employers with even one employee are required to carry workers compensation. Your property policy does not provide workers comp. If an employee is injured and you don't have workers comp, you face both the full cost of the injury and potential regulatory penalties.

⚠️
"Independent Contractor" Is Not a Shield

Many horse property operators believe calling someone an independent contractor exempts them from workers comp requirements. State labor departments and courts look at the actual working relationship — not the label — and frequently reclassify workers as employees. If you control when, how, and where someone works on your property, they may be an employee regardless of how you're paying them.

4. Business Income and Boarding Revenue

When a fire destroys your barn, your boarding business stops. Your property policy replaces the barn (at whatever limit you had). It does not replace the $8,000/month in boarding revenue you've lost for the 14 months it takes to rebuild. Business income coverage — also called business interruption — is a separate endorsement or policy that must be specifically purchased and must reflect your actual revenue.

5. Commercial Equipment and Farm Machinery

Tractors, skid steers, arena drags, manure spreaders, horse trailers used for commercial transport — standard property policies either exclude commercial equipment outright or provide sublimits (often $5,000–$15,000) that bear no relationship to actual equipment values. A $45,000 tractor is not insured under your homeowners policy. It requires a scheduled equipment floater or a farm and ranch policy.

6. Professional Liability / Errors and Omissions

A client's horse is injured during training and the client believes negligent training caused the injury. A veterinarian recommends against a treatment you provided. A horse you sold has a health issue the buyer claims you failed to disclose. Professional liability (errors and omissions) insurance covers claims arising from your professional services — it is not included in any property policy and must be purchased separately.

7. Equine Mortality for Client Horses

If a horse in your care dies — from any cause — and you don't have CCC coverage, you may be legally liable to the owner for the horse's value. You also cannot collect mortality insurance on a horse you don't own. The horse's owner must carry their own mortality policy. Your boarding agreement should address this explicitly.

8. Product Liability (Feed, Supplements, Treatments)

If you provide feed, supplements, medications, or any product to horses in your care and a horse is harmed — you face product liability exposure. A homeowners policy does not cover product liability arising from business activities.

9. Advertising and Personal Injury Liability

A competitor claims your marketing materials defame their operation. A former employee claims wrongful termination. A vendor dispute becomes a lawsuit. Business disputes and commercial torts are not covered under personal property policies.

10. Trailer Liability During Commercial Transport

If you haul horses commercially — for clients, for a fee, or as part of a training program — your personal auto policy's trailer coverage may not apply. Commercial horse transport requires either a commercial auto policy or a specifically endorsed personal auto policy. In most states, if money changes hands for transport, it's commercial activity.

11. Damage to Rented or Borrowed Equipment

Property in your care, custody, or control that belongs to others — whether borrowed equipment, a client's trailer stored on your property, or leased machinery — is excluded from your standard property coverage. Damage to these items requires a separate inland marine or equipment floater policy.

12. Cyber Liability and Data Breach

Modern boarding operations collect client payment information, store veterinary records, and may process electronic payments. A data breach exposing client financial or personal information is not covered under any property policy. Cyber liability coverage is available as a separate policy or endorsement.

When You Need a Farm and Ranch Policy Instead

A farm and ranch policy is a commercial insurance product that combines dwelling, structures, liability, equipment, livestock, and business coverages into a single policy designed for agricultural and equestrian operations. You likely need one if:

  • You receive income from boarding, lessons, training, or breeding
  • You have employees — even part-time barn help
  • You own commercial equipment (tractors, commercial trailers)
  • Your property is valued above $1M total
  • You have more than 5–6 horses
  • Clients, students, or the public regularly access your property
  • You provide any professional equestrian services for compensation
✓
The Right Policy Stack for a Boarding/Training Operation

Farm & Ranch policy (dwelling + structures + farm equipment) + Commercial General Liability with equine endorsement + Care-Custody-Control coverage + Workers Compensation + Business Income coverage + Equine Mortality for owned horses. A specialist agent can often package these into a single manageable policy.

How to Find an Equine Insurance Specialist

Standard homeowners agents do not have access to the equine specialty market and often do not understand the nuances of horse property coverage. The carriers that write comprehensive equine commercial coverage — Markel, Great American, KBHS, West Bend, Nationwide Agribusiness — require agents with equine specialty appointments.

Ask any agent you're considering: Do you write Markel equine policies? Do you have farm and ranch markets? Have you written CCC coverage before? If they hesitate or don't know what CCC means, find someone else.
