Wildfire Risk & Horse Property Insurance

The most financially devastating event a horse property can face — and the coverage that most owners discover is inadequate only after their barn is ash.

Why Wildfire Is Different From Every Other Property Risk

A house fire, a break-in, a hailstorm — these are isolated events affecting one structure. Wildfire is different in every meaningful way. It arrives with almost no warning, moves faster than horses can be evacuated, destroys every structure simultaneously, eliminates fencing across entire pastures, contaminates water systems, and can render land uninhabitable for months. The total loss scenario isn't the worst case on a horse property — it is the common case.

The insurance implications compound: multiple structures claim simultaneously, replacement costs are elevated because every contractor in the region is booked, debris removal bills arrive before reconstruction can begin, horses may be lost or injured during evacuation, and if you operated a boarding or training business — that income has stopped entirely.

The Carrier Withdrawal Crisis

From 2020 through 2024, every major admitted carrier in California significantly reduced or eliminated their writing of horse properties in high fire-risk zones. State Farm, Allstate, Farmers, and others have either non-renewed existing policies or stopped writing new business in WUI (wildland-urban interface) zones that cover the majority of California horse country — Sonoma, Napa, Santa Barbara, San Diego, Riverside, and others.

Arizona, Colorado, Oregon, Washington, and New Mexico are following the same pattern. Carriers are using wildfire risk scoring models (CoreLogic, Verisk, and proprietary tools) to identify high-risk properties and either surcharge them significantly or decline to renew. The result is a rapidly shrinking admitted market and a growing surplus lines market where premiums can run 2–5x standard rates.

⚠️
Non-Renewal Notice: What to Do Immediately

If you receive a non-renewal notice from your current carrier, you have a window — typically 60–90 days — to find replacement coverage. Do not let the policy lapse. A gap in coverage during wildfire season can be catastrophic. Contact an equine insurance specialist, not a standard homeowners agent, to access the surplus lines and specialty markets.

What Wildfire Coverage Actually Pays — And What It Doesn't

Covered Under a Standard Policy (Usually)

  • Dwelling structure — at coverage limit (which may be inadequate)
  • Other structures — typically 10% of dwelling limit (almost always inadequate for barn replacement)
  • Personal property — subject to limits and depreciation
  • Additional living expense — if you're displaced from the residence
  • Fencing — often a sublimit ($5,000–$25,000 on most policies)

Not Covered Under a Standard Property Policy

  • Horse mortality — horses are not property; requires separate equine mortality insurance
  • Business income / boarding revenue loss — excluded by business activity exclusion
  • Workers compensation for employees injured during evacuation
  • Commercial liability for client horses that die in your care
  • Equipment and farm machinery above standard limits
  • Evacuation costs and emergency horse transport
  • Smoke damage to hay, feed, and consumables
  • Temporary boarding costs while your facility is rebuilt

The single biggest issue in wildfire claims on horse properties is underinsurance on structures. Here is why it happens:

Post-wildfire, replacement costs surge further — labor shortages, material demand spikes, and contractor availability compress after a regional fire event. What cost $120,000 to build before the fire may cost $180,000 to rebuild 12 months later.

ACV vs. Replacement Cost — The Dispute That Defines Every Claim

Actual Cash Value (ACV) pays what the structure was worth at the time of the loss — replacement cost minus depreciation. A 20-year-old wood barn with replacement value of $120,000 might have an ACV of $40,000–$60,000 after depreciation. The owner must absorb the gap.

Replacement Cost Value (RCV) pays what it actually costs to rebuild to the same quality. This is the coverage you need. Not all equine property policies write RCV on other structures (barns, arenas, sheds) even when they write it on the dwelling. Verify every structure on your policy is written at RCV — not ACV.

📋
Extended and Guaranteed Replacement Cost

Extended replacement cost (typically 120–150% of coverage limit) and guaranteed replacement cost (carrier pays actual rebuild cost regardless of limit) are available from some carriers on dwellings. They are rarely available on farm structures. If you can get extended replacement on your barn, take it.

Wildfire Coverage by State — What You're Actually Working With

California

The most difficult admitted market in the country. State Farm, Allstate, Farmers, and others have significantly pulled back. The California FAIR Plan (Fire Insurance Exchange) is the insurer of last resort — it provides basic fire coverage only, with no liability, no business income, and structure limits that may be inadequate. Horse properties in fire-risk zip codes often layer FAIR Plan fire coverage with a separate Difference in Conditions (DIC) policy from the surplus lines market.

Arizona

The admitted market remains viable in most of Arizona, but WUI properties in Verde Valley, Prescott area, and rural Yavapai and Coconino counties face carrier scrutiny. Brush clearance requirements and fire-resistant construction can help maintain admitted market access. Surplus lines carriers are increasingly active for high-risk-zone properties.

Colorado

The Marshall Fire (2021) fundamentally changed the Colorado admitted market. Jefferson, Boulder, and El Paso counties have seen significant non-renewals. Horse properties along the Front Range and in mountain foothills face the same carrier pullback seen in California several years earlier.

Texas

Less constrained than western states but not without wildfire exposure. The Texas FAIR Plan exists but is rarely needed except in extreme western Texas. Most horse properties can access admitted carriers, though premiums have increased significantly in brush-country regions.

Oregon, Washington, Idaho, Montana

Pacific Northwest carriers are actively reassessing wildfire exposure. Properties in eastern Oregon, eastern Washington, and western Montana face increasing restrictions. The admitted market is tightening but has not yet reached California's level of restriction.

Loss of Horses in a Wildfire — The Coverage That Doesn't Exist in Your Property Policy

Your horse property insurance covers structures. It does not cover horses. Full stop. If a wildfire kills, injures, or causes you to euthanize your horses — that loss is not covered by your property policy under any circumstances.

Equine mortality insurance is a separate product. It is available from specialty carriers — Markel, Great American, KBHS, and others — and must be purchased before the loss. Mortality policies typically cover death from accident, illness, and fire. They require a current health certificate and veterinary exam for high-value horses.

The hard timeline reality: if your horses die in a wildfire, you have a limited window (often 24–72 hours post-death) to file a mortality claim and meet documentation requirements. In the chaos of an active wildfire emergency, most owners miss these deadlines and lose all recovery.

🚨
Mortality Claim Deadlines Are Not Flexible

Most equine mortality policies require notification within 24–72 hours of death or humane destruction. Save your insurance carrier's emergency contact number in your phone before wildfire season. Program it now. In an active evacuation, you will not be able to find this information.

Evacuation Planning and Insurance Implications

Insurance covers what you lose — it does not replace proper preparation. Evacuation planning directly affects your insurance outcomes in several ways. Documenting your property with photos and video (structures, equipment, horses, serial numbers) before a fire creates the evidence base for a strong claim. Maintaining current appraisals on high-value horses and equipment gives you claim documentation. Knowing your policy limits in advance lets you make coverage corrections before a loss rather than discovering gaps in the aftermath.

A wildfire evacuation plan for a horse property should include: trailer capacity and loading order, pre-identified evacuation destinations with stall availability, documentation storage (policy numbers, vet records, horse registration papers) in a fireproof location or cloud backup, and contacts for emergency equine evacuation assistance in your county.

How to Get the Right Wildfire Coverage

Work with an equine insurance specialist — not a standard homeowners agent — who has access to the farm and ranch and surplus lines markets. The questions that matter:

  • Is each structure covered at full replacement cost value, individually scheduled?
  • Does debris removal coverage equal at least 25% of structure value?
  • Is there an extended replacement cost provision on structures?
  • Are fences and gates covered at actual replacement value (not a sublimit)?
  • Is farm equipment and machinery scheduled at current values?
  • If I operate a business, is business income included or excluded?
  • Does the policy write on RCV or ACV for barns and outbuildings?
  • What is the carrier's wildfire risk score for my property?
