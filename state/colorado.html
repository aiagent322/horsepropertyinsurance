Horse Property Insurance in Colorado

Colorado's horse property insurance market is defined by two converging challenges: increasing wildfire risk across the Front Range foothills and mountain communities, and a robust hail season that affects the eastern plains and Denver metro properties each summer. The state has a large and active equestrian community spanning competitive show horses along the Front Range, working ranch horses on the eastern plains, and mountain pleasure horses in the resort communities.

Front Range Wildfire Exposure

Communities from Fort Collins through Colorado Springs along the I-25 corridor have significant wildfire interface exposure. Major fires including the Marshall Fire of 2021 — which destroyed hundreds of structures in Boulder County — demonstrated that wildfire risk extends into suburban and semi-rural horse property areas, not just remote mountain locations. Horse property owners in these areas should:

  • Review wildfire exclusions and sublimits carefully
  • Maintain current replacement cost coverage — rebuilding costs in Colorado have risen sharply
  • Document ember-resistant construction features and defensible space

Hail Risk on the Eastern Plains

Colorado experiences some of the most severe hail in the country, particularly on the eastern plains and in the Denver metro area. Arena roofs, barn roofs, and covered facilities are vulnerable. Metal roofing with Class 4 impact resistance ratings may qualify for premium discounts.

Key Coverage Considerations for Colorado Horse Properties

  • Wildfire coverage terms for Front Range and mountain properties
  • Hail-resistant roofing discounts for new or recently re-roofed structures
  • Altitude considerations — some mountain properties have limited carrier availability
  • Winter storm coverage for facilities — roof collapse from snow load is a real risk
  • Water rights infrastructure — ditches, water shares, and irrigation equipment
